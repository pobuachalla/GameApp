GK Shot-Stopping · Save Score 1–5

The five points, with examples

Each score answers: how much danger remains after the keeper's first touch?

Key idea

The save score measures residual danger — not difficulty. Shot difficulty is captured separately by Intensity. This separation is what makes the matrix work.

Save score — quick reference

Score One-liner Residual danger Secondary action
1 Beaten — goal in Goal scored Not applicable
2 Rebound in the danger zone High Auto-prompted
3 Loose / 50-50 ball in front of goal Medium Auto-prompted
4 Pushed clear — over, wide, or to teammate Low Optional
5 Caught / smothered — full control None Not needed
"Where is the ball one second after the keeper's first touch?" In the net → 1  ·  Loose in the danger zone → 2  ·  50-50 in front of goal → 3  ·  Out / cleared safely → 4  ·  In the keeper's hands → 5
Intensity — difficulty of the shot
5
Exceptional — top-corner strike, point-blank, or a shot the keeper could not have anticipated. Scoring was the expected outcome.
4
Difficult — low drive, fast ground shot, or a significant deflection. Required meaningful skill to stop.
3
Moderate — standard shot within the keeper's range. Expected to be dealt with, but not trivial.
2
Routine — straightforward attempt at the keeper's body or near at hand. Comfortable to hold.
1
Minimal — tame attempt, weak shot, or speculative long-range effort with little pace or placement.
Secondary Outcome — what happened next?
1
Opposition scored — the rebound was converted. Adds −2 to the event value.
2
Danger continues — opposition retained ball in a threatening position. Adds −1.
3
Neutral — ball went dead (wide, over the bar) or situation reset. No adjustment.
4
Team retained — own team regained possession. Adds +1.
5
Attack launched — team immediately transitioned into attack from the save. Adds +2.
